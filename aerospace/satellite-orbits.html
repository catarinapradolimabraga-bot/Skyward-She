Satellite orbits — LEO, MEO, GEO & special orbits

By Catarina Prado — Published on December 7, 2025

Satellites occupy a variety of orbital regimes, each chosen to match mission needs such as coverage, resolution, latency and stability. From low Earth imaging to geostationary communications, each orbit offers unique advantages and engineering challenges. This article explains the structure and purpose of the most important orbits used today.

1. Low Earth Orbit (LEO)

Altitude: 160–2,000 km
Period: ~90–120 minutes
Velocity: ~7.8 km/s

LEO is the most active orbital region, hosting remote sensing satellites, scientific missions, the ISS, and modern broadband constellations. It provides high-resolution imagery, low communication latency, and relatively low launch requirements — but suffers from atmospheric drag and growing congestion.

2. Medium Earth Orbit (MEO)

Altitude: 2,000–35,786 km
Period: several hours

Navigation constellations such as GPS, Galileo and GLONASS operate in MEO. Its altitude enables global coverage with fewer satellites and offers a stable, low-drag environment for precise timing and positioning missions.

3. Geostationary Orbit (GEO)

Altitude: 35,786 km
Period: 24 hours (Earth rotation)

GEO satellites appear fixed in the sky. This makes them ideal for communications, broadcast television, and weather monitoring. GEO offers unmatched coverage but requires high launch energy and results in higher communication latency.

4. Sun-synchronous Orbit (SSO)

SSO is a specialized near-polar LEO orbit that maintains constant local solar time for each ground pass. This enables imaging missions to compare lighting conditions consistently, which is essential for climate science, remote sensing and environmental monitoring.

5. Molniya Orbit

A highly elliptical, 12-hour orbit designed for high-latitude coverage. Because GEO satellites sit low on the horizon for northern regions, Molniya orbit provides extended dwell time over Arctic and sub-Arctic locations — ideal for communications and early-warning systems.

6. Tundra Orbit

Similar to Molniya but with a 24-hour period. Tundra orbits provide GEO-like coverage for high latitudes, with long dwell time and flexible positioning. Used for services such as satellite radio in North America.

7. Orbital end-of-life: disposal & mitigation

GEO satellites are boosted into a “graveyard orbit” hundreds of kilometers above GEO to prevent interference. LEO satellites typically deorbit to burn up in the atmosphere. With rising satellite numbers, debris mitigation is a central engineering requirement for all new missions.
