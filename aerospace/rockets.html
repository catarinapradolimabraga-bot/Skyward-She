How rockets work — detailed

By Catarina Prado — Published on December 7, 2025

Rocket propulsion is one of the most elegant demonstrations of physics in motion. From Newton’s laws to Tsiolkovsky’s equation, every launch is a blend of chemistry, thermodynamics, structural engineering, and trajectory design. This article breaks down the essential principles behind how modern rockets generate thrust and reach orbit.

1. Newton’s Third Law: the foundation

The entire concept of rocket propulsion comes from Newton’s Third Law: “For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.” A rocket accelerates by expelling mass backward at extremely high velocity. In space, where there is no air to push against, this principle becomes even clearer — the rocket pushes only on its own exhaust.

2. Combustion: creating energy and pressure

Inside the combustion chamber, fuel and oxidizer react violently, producing hot, high-pressure gases. The chamber is engineered to withstand extreme temperatures and maintain stable burning, while its pressure forces the gases outward through the nozzle.

3. Nozzle design: turning pressure into velocity

Most rockets use a de Laval nozzle, a convergent–divergent shape that accelerates gases to supersonic speeds. At the throat, the gas reaches Mach 1. As it expands into the diverging section, it accelerates even further, creating the high-velocity exhaust jet responsible for thrust.

4. Specific impulse: measuring efficiency

Specific impulse (Isp) tells us how effectively a rocket uses its propellant. Higher Isp means more velocity for the same amount of fuel. Examples:

  • ~450 s — liquid hydrogen + liquid oxygen (very efficient, used in upper stages)
  • ~300 s — RP-1 kerosene + LOX (common in first stages)
  • ~200–260 s — solid propellants

5. Tsiolkovsky’s rocket equation & staging

The famous equation:

Δv = vₑ · ln(m₀ / m_f)

shows why multi-stage rockets are essential. To achieve orbital velocity, a vehicle must shed empty mass along the way. Each discarded stage increases the efficiency of the remaining vehicle.

6. The gravity turn

Rockets do not go “straight up.” After clearing the atmosphere, they gradually tilt to begin accelerating horizontally — a maneuver called a gravity turn. For low Earth orbit, the required horizontal velocity is about 7.8 km/s.

7. Propellant choices

Each propellant family offers different advantages:

  • Liquid hydrogen — very high Isp, very low density
  • RP-1 kerosene — excellent density, easier storage
  • Solids — simple, reliable, but cannot be throttled

8. Real-world constraints

Engineering a rocket means balancing structure, heat protection, engine stresses, guidance systems, and mass constraints — all while ensuring reliability. Even minor improvements in mass fraction or nozzle efficiency can dramatically improve performance.
