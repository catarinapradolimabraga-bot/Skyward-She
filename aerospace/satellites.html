Satellites & telemetry

By Catarina Prado — Published on December 7, 2025

Satellites are the silent infrastructure of the modern world. From weather prediction to navigation systems, scientific research and global communication, satellites rely on orbital mechanics, onboard instruments and telemetry — the continuous stream of data linking spacecraft to Earth.

1. What exactly is a satellite?

A satellite is any object that orbits a planet because gravity pulls it inward while its forward velocity keeps it moving sideways. Natural satellites (like the Moon) share the same physics as artificial satellites used for communications, imaging, navigation and deep-space exploration.

2. Types of orbits and their purposes

Low Earth Orbit (LEO)

Altitude: 160–2,000 km. Used by imaging satellites, ISS, Starlink. Offers high resolution and low latency but requires large constellations.

Medium Earth Orbit (MEO)

Altitude: 2,000–35,786 km. Home to navigation systems like GPS, Galileo and GLONASS.

Geostationary Orbit (GEO)

At 35,786 km, satellites orbit at the same rotational speed as Earth, appearing fixed in the sky. Ideal for weather, communication and broadcast missions.

Special orbits

  • Sun-synchronous: constant lighting conditions for imaging missions.
  • Molniya: long dwell time over high latitudes.
  • Polar: covers the entire Earth as the planet rotates beneath the orbit.

3. What payloads do satellites carry?

Satellites are divided into two main components:

The payload

  • Imagers and multispectral cameras
  • Radar (SAR)
  • Communication transponders
  • Scientific instruments
  • Navigation payloads with atomic clocks

The bus (spacecraft body)

  • Power systems (solar arrays and batteries)
  • Attitude control (reaction wheels, magnetorquers, star trackers)
  • Propulsion
  • Thermal management
  • Command and data handling computers

4. Telemetry: how satellites communicate with Earth

Telemetry is the continuous stream of health, status and science data sent from a satellite to Earth. Engineers rely on telemetry to track:

  • Battery state and power production
  • Temperatures across the spacecraft
  • Attitude and angular rates
  • Propellant levels
  • Payload activity
  • Error logs and computer health

How telemetry works

  • The satellite encodes data into radio signals.
  • Antennas transmit the signals to Earth.
  • Ground stations receive and decode the information.
  • Engineers analyze it and send command uplinks back to space.

Common telemetry bands

  • UHF/VHF: small satellites, amateur missions
  • S-band: science telemetry and command
  • X-band: Earth observation, high data rate
  • Ka-band: very high bandwidth communication

5. Attitude control: keeping the satellite pointed correctly

Orientation is essential for imaging, communication and solar power. Spacecraft use:

  • Reaction wheels
  • Magnetorquers
  • Thrusters
  • Star trackers

6. Why telemetry is essential

Telemetry enables long-duration space missions. Voyager 1, launched in 1977, still transmits data from interstellar space. Mission control relies on telemetry to assess spacecraft health, schedule maneuvers, prevent failures and optimize mission success.
