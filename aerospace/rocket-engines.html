Rocket engines — combustion, nozzles & efficiency

By Catarina Prado — Published on December 7, 2025

Rocket engines are among the most powerful machines ever developed. Unlike air-breathing engines, rockets carry both fuel and oxidizer, allowing them to operate in the vacuum of space. Their design combines fluid dynamics, thermodynamics, combustion chemistry and advanced materials to convert stored chemical energy into high-velocity exhaust and thrust.

1. The physics of thrust

Rockets operate according to Newton’s Third Law: when propellant is expelled backward, the rocket accelerates forward. The basic thrust equation is:

Thrust = mass flow rate × exhaust velocity

Engineers increase thrust by optimizing propellant flow rate (ṁ) and exhaust velocity (Ve).

2. Combustion chamber

The combustion chamber mixes fuel and oxidizer and burns them at extreme temperatures—often above 3,000 K. The chamber must maintain high pressure and withstand thermal loads as it feeds hot gas into the nozzle.

Common propellant types

  • Liquid engines: RP-1/LOX, LH₂/LOX, hypergolics (MMH + N₂O₄)
  • Solid engines: fuel and oxidizer mixed into a single grain
  • Hybrid engines: liquid oxidizer + solid fuel

Higher chamber pressure typically improves performance by increasing exhaust velocity.

3. Nozzles: converting heat into momentum

Most rocket engines use a de Laval nozzle with a converging section, throat and diverging section. Gas reaches Mach 1 at the throat and expands supersonically through the diverging area, gaining velocity and producing thrust.

Nozzle length and expansion ratio depend on the operating environment:

  • Sea-level nozzles: shorter, optimized for higher ambient pressure
  • Vacuum nozzles: longer, wider, optimized for low pressure

4. Efficiency: specific impulse (Isp)

Specific impulse is the key measure of rocket efficiency:

Isp = thrust produced per unit of propellant consumption

Higher Isp means the engine gets more thrust from less propellant.

Typical Isp values

  • Solid boosters: ~250 s
  • RP-1/LOX engines: 300–330 s
  • LH₂/LOX engines: 430–465 s
  • Ion engines: 3,000+ s

5. Engine cycles

Gas-generator cycle

Burns a small amount of propellant in a separate chamber to drive the turbopump. Simple but less efficient. Used in Merlin engines.

Staged-combustion cycle

A preburner produces hot gas to drive the turbine, and the exhaust is fed into the main chamber. Extremely efficient and high pressure. Used in Raptor, RD-180, RS-25.

Pressure-fed systems

Tanks are pressurized with inert gas instead of using turbopumps. Reliable and simple; used in spacecraft maneuvering thrusters.

6. Cooling and materials

Rocket engines withstand some of the harshest thermal environments in engineering. Techniques include:

  • Regenerative cooling
  • Ablative cooling
  • Transpiration cooling
  • High-temperature alloys (Inconel, niobium, rhenium)
