Atmospheric reentry — heating, plasma & shielding

By Catarina Prado — Published on December 7, 2025

Atmospheric reentry is one of the most extreme phases of any space mission. Returning spacecraft face intense heating, plasma formation, shock waves and high g-loads. Understanding how heat is generated and how thermal protection systems work is essential for designing capsules, probes and reusable vehicles.

1. Why spacecraft heat up during reentry

Spacecraft do not heat primarily due to friction. The dominant mechanism is compression heating: as the vehicle travels at hypersonic speed, it compresses atmospheric gases in front of it. According to thermodynamic laws, compressing a gas raises its temperature, generating a superheated shock layer that can exceed 5,000–10,000 K.

These temperatures are high enough to ionize air molecules, forming a plasma sheath around the vehicle — hot, electrically charged gas that radiates heat toward the spacecraft surface.

2. Plasma formation and communications blackout

The ionized gas surrounding the spacecraft absorbs radio waves, causing a temporary loss of communications. Apollo missions experienced a 3–4 minute blackout; modern capsules such as SpaceX Dragon and Soyuz experience similar effects during peak heating.

As the plasma cools, radio transparency returns and communication is restored.

3. Deceleration loads and g-forces

Reentry slows spacecraft extremely quickly, producing significant g-forces:

  • Apollo: 6–7 g
  • Soyuz: up to 8–9 g
  • SpaceX Crew Dragon: ~4–5 g
  • Space Shuttle: ~1.5 g (thanks to aerodynamic lift)

Capsules follow steep, ballistic trajectories, while lifting vehicles spread heating over a longer path, reducing peak deceleration.

4. Thermal Protection Systems (TPS)

There are several TPS approaches, each suited to different mission types:

Ablative Shielding

Used in Apollo, Orion and sample-return missions. Material chars, melts and vaporizes, carrying heat away with the ablated mass. Examples include AVCOAT and PICA/PICA-X.

Reusable Insulation

Silica tiles and composite materials endure high temperatures while remaining lightweight. Used by the Space Shuttle and emerging reusable spacecraft.

Metallic Heat Shields

Made of high-temperature metals such as Inconel or titanium alloys. Used in lifting-body vehicles like Dream Chaser.

Transpiration Cooling

Experimental method where coolant is pushed through porous surfaces to cool the structure from within.

5. Designing reentry trajectories

Key parameters include:

Flight Path Angle

Too steep: extreme heating and g-loads. Too shallow: risk of skipping off the atmosphere.

Bank Angle Modulation

Lifting vehicles roll to control lift, drag and descent rate — used by the Space Shuttle and SpaceX Starship.

Drag Profile

Capsules rely mainly on drag; lifting vehicles use a mix of drag and controlled lift.

6. Types of reentry

Suborbital (< 3 km/s)

Lower heating; used by vehicles like Blue Origin's New Shepard.

Orbital (~7–8 km/s)

Typical for ISS return missions and reusable vehicles.

Lunar or interplanetary (11–13 km/s)

The most demanding type. NASA’s Stardust probe reached 12.9 km/s — the fastest Earth reentry ever recorded.
