Orbits Explained

By Catarina Prado — Published on December 7, 2025

Orbital mechanics is one of the most refined areas of aerospace engineering — a discipline where elegance, mathematics, and precision come together. Understanding how orbits work means understanding gravity, velocity, energy, and the subtle geometry that defines spacecraft trajectories.

1. Gravity as a constant pull

A spacecraft in orbit is not floating — it is constantly falling toward Earth while moving sideways fast enough that it never reaches the surface. Newton’s cannonball analogy describes this perfectly: with the right horizontal velocity, the planet curves away beneath the object at the same rate it falls.

2. Circular and elliptical orbits

Kepler showed that orbits follow ellipses, with the central body at one focus. Circular orbits maintain constant altitude, while elliptical orbits vary between perigee (closest point) and apogee (farthest point).

In Low Earth Orbit (LEO), spacecraft travel around 7.8 km/s, completing a full revolution in about 90 minutes.

3. Orbital energy: bound and unbound motion

Engineers describe orbits using total mechanical energy. If energy is negative → the spacecraft is bound to Earth. If it reaches zero or positive → it escapes.

Escape velocity at Earth’s surface is approximately 11.2 km/s, representing the energy needed to break free of the planet’s gravitational well.

4. Orbital velocity and altitude

Lower orbits require higher speeds; higher orbits require lower speeds. This is why the International Space Station orbits rapidly, while geostationary satellites take 24 hours to complete one revolution.

5. Orbital maneuvers

To change an orbit, spacecraft perform velocity changes known as Δv (delta-v). The most efficient maneuver is the Hohmann transfer, a two-burn technique for moving between circular orbits.

  • Bi-elliptic transfers
  • Plane changes
  • Gravity assists

6. Perturbations

Real orbits drift due to Earth’s uneven gravity field, atmospheric drag, the Moon, the Sun, and solar radiation pressure. Engineers counter these effects through routine station-keeping maneuvers.
