How I Started My Research Journey at 13

By Catarina

My research journey began earlier than most people expect. At 13, I was already deeply curious about how the world works — especially anything related to physics, space, and engineering. I didn’t have a lab or professional equipment, but I had something even more important: curiosity and the desire to learn.

Discovering My Passion

It all started with simple questions. Why do planes fly? How do satellites stay in orbit? Why are rockets shaped the way they are?

Every question pushed me deeper into aerospace engineering. I realized how much research depends not on resources, but on asking good questions and being willing to explore.

Science Olympiads: My First “Research Steps”

Participating in olympiads was my first real contact with scientific thinking. They taught me how to analyze problems, think critically, and communicate solutions. Even without noticing, I was already practicing research skills:

  • breaking questions into smaller steps,
  • finding patterns,
  • and building explanations based on evidence.

Starting My Own Projects

I created small research-style projects on my own: analyzing flight mechanics, comparing rocket designs, and exploring the physics behind space travel. They weren’t “formal” projects, but they trained me to think like a researcher — and that was the whole point.

Advice for Other Girls Starting Young

If you're also beginning your research journey early, here are my best tips:

  • Start with topics you love — passion makes everything easier.
  • Don’t worry about having lab equipment.
  • Ask lots of questions — research starts with curiosity.
  • Use free resources online; the internet is your most powerful lab.
  • Document everything — this becomes your first research portfolio.

Today, my dream is to become an aerospace engineer and inspire more girls to join STEM. Starting early made me confident, curious, and ready for bigger challenges.
